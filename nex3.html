 Read Me!


Tools Used

- Plastic Pick (For Ribbon Cables)
- X-Acto Knife
- Philps Head Screw Driver


Important

- Read this whole thing before you start. That last thing you want is to get halfway though this and realize you don't understand something or missed a step.

- Most of the photos you could need are here. If you've ever worked with small electronics before doing this mod is easy, if not just follow the pictures and where some are missing just use common sense (i.e. if you see a screw that isn't listed, remove it)

- Keep track of everything you remove, where it came from, and the order is should be put back together in. This means being aware of where specific screws were. Some screws are different lengths, you don't want to put them in the wrong hole.



My organization system. For all the PCBs and plates I drew a small diagram so I knew where each screw went on it.


 Instructions



Walkthrough


The first thing you want to do, as might be expected, is to remove all the external screws. There are a number visble around the body however, a handful are hidden underneath the flash cover. Once all the external screws are out, carefully pull off the back plastic plate.


Once that is done the camera should like this. From here there are only three visible screws and a single ribbon cable. Remove the screws and disconnect the ribbon cable.


The command wheel is just held in with metal tabs, unhook it from it's metal plate and remove the screen unit.


Following the removal of the screen you will be greeted with the layed circuit boards underneath. Most of the steps from here on out are easy. It is just a careful game of trying to find all the screws and ribbon cables. On this very top board there are a good number of both.


Make sure to check under the plastic barrier for cables and under those cables for screws. When you pull something away do it slowly and stop if you feel any resistance. Also, the microphone is attached to this board. It sits nestled in the case and easy comes out if you pull it. (Black square in the bottom left)


Now onto the board underneath the one you just took out. This is the board that the sensor actually sits against, hence the heat sink. Once again, go around the board and remove any of the screws that you can see now and detach the ribbon cables. Be careful with the bottom leftmost ribbon cable as it dips under the board.
This is also the time where you have to remove the right circuit board if you didn't already remove it with the previous one.


After doing all of that you will be able to remove that board and remove the even smaller board that the sensor is attached to, leaving the camera with a gaping hole.


There's only a few small screws that hold the senor on here, no more ribbon cables. While you should be careful the sensor and its board, you do want it to work after this afterall, it doesn't matter if you get fingerprints on the glass covering the sensor since that has come off anyways.


Voilà, you now have the heart of the camera in your hand. If you look at the senor from the side you will see the glass that sits over the sensor and the glue that holds it in place.


You want to get some small tool, I used an X-Acto knife, that can get under the glue layer and pry it off of the plastic frame around the sensor. If you look at the photo you can see that I didn't get under it on my first time and I ended up just cracking the glass. I guess, in theory, you could leave that glue layer in place and only get rid of the glass but it's much easier to do the whole thing and make sure the sensor is clean.


Once you're done with that you will be left with this. If you pulled off the glue nicely there should be very little residue on the glass that surrounds the sensor. I had a little bit left but left it in place as you have to be very careful cleaning the glass here since this is the one on the sensor. As such, when handling this keep your fingers away from it, you don't fingerprints in your photo, do you?


The next step is to just do all of that again, but in reverse. Reattached the sensor to its board, that board to the second one, and then the final two baords that you could see when you took off the screen. It's a good practice to just loosly assemble everything and test it before you fully reassemble the whole thing. You're on your own for troubleshooting any issues you may have but it will be ever so slightly less painful if you don't have to take the whole outershell off again.


After you test everything, and hoepfully it all works, you will have reassemble everything back into its home. The hardest part, for me, was the screen. I hadn't really paid attention to how it came off and with the narrow gap that you have to reattach the ribbon cable, it took a lot of fighting to get it to go back. In my frustation, and because it seemed like it was the problem, I ended up dremmeling off part of the top bracket that holds the screen in place in a vain attempt to get it to fit. It didn't actually help it go back together but hopefully that encourages you to pay attention how you took it off.


If you've done everything right it hopefully worked fine when you tested it and you can now slide everything back into place and screw in the final screws. What you'll be left with is a full spectrum IR camera. You can shoot it like this if you really wanted, or just place around with anything you have to see if it gives off IR, but to get the most out of the camera you will need to buy IR filters.
